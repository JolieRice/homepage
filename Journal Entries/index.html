Handmade Web Journal

Thoughts and reflections from class readings

Laurel Schwulst – What is a Website

Reading Schwulst’s “What is a Website” completely reshaped how I think about the internet as a creative space. As a graphic design major, I’m used to thinking about layout, typography, and branding, but Schwulst encourages seeing websites as personal, poetic, and even alive. I appreciated how she compared websites to gardens, temples, and playgrounds, it reminded me that websites don’t have to be rigid grids of content. They can feel like zines, diaries, or pieces of art. This text made me more excited about the possibilities of handmade web design, especially how it can reflect personal identity or mood in ways that templates and standardized platforms often can’t. It also made me want to slow down and consider the tone and “spirit” of the sites I make, not just the function.

J. R. Carpenter – A Handmade Web

Carpenter’s writing really emphasized the value of imperfection and intimacy in web design. I often feel pressure to make things look polished and professional, but this text reminded me that the web can also be a place for experimentation and personal expression. I liked how Carpenter tied the handmade web to zine culture, prioritizing the maker's voice over commercial slickness. It was refreshing to hear someone argue for websites that feel human and unique, especially in contrast to the sameness of social media and template driven design. This reading encouraged me to think of my code as craft, and that even small choices can carry meaning.

Taeyoon Choi – Hello World!

Choi’s essay made me think more deeply about the connection between code and community. I often approach websites as visual systems, but this reading emphasized the importance of the people behind the screens, their intentions, accessibility needs, and how they experience technology. Choi’s inclusive and patient approach to teaching code reminded me that handmade web work doesn’t have to be intimidating, it can be slow, collaborative, and rooted in care. I also loved the small, playful illustrations throughout the piece, they added warmth and personality, and showed how visual elements can support storytelling in a way that feels human. It reminded me that design can be both simple and expressive, even in the context of coding.

Callum Copley – A Friend is Writing

Reading A Friend is Writing felt less like reading and more like eavesdropping on a collective thought process. The overlapping messages popping up across the screen created a sense of urgency, chaos, and connection all at once. I’m used to thinking about clarity and hierarchy, but this experience challenged that. It felt intentionally disorienting, like the format was mimicking the noise of constant digital communication. I started to think about how design can be used not just to organize content, but to evoke emotion and reflect social realities. The web doesn’t always have to be clean and linear, it can feel like a crowded room or a conversation thread. This reading reminded me that storytelling online doesn’t have to follow a single voice or direction. It can be messy, collaborative, and alive.

Becca Abbe – The Internet’s Back-to-the-Land Movement

This piece made me reflect on how digital spaces are starting to mirror the values of the DIY and back-to-the-land movements, rejecting mass production in favor of something slower, smaller, and more intentional. I often feel caught between wanting to make polished, professional work and craving a more personal, expressive way of creating. Abbe’s writing made it clear that there’s a growing desire to reclaim the web as a place for community, experimentation, and self-reliance, much like a handmade object. It’s inspiring to see how people are building their own sites as alternatives to corporate platforms. This article encouraged me to think about my own web presence as a kind of digital home, something I can shape, decorate, and share on my own terms.

Frank Chimero – The Good Room

Chimero’s metaphor of the web as a “room” really stuck with me. It made me think about how we design online spaces not just to display information, but to make people feel welcomed, curious, and inspired. I’m always considering layout and visual flow, but this essay pushed me to think more about mood and atmosphere. What kind of “room” am I creating when I build a site? Is it cozy, open, cluttered, cold? Chimero’s critique of how the internet has shifted toward utility over beauty made me reflect on how handmade, personal sites can reintroduce a sense of care and presence. It reminded me that design isn't just about solving problems, it’s also about creating meaningful experiences, and sometimes, simply making space for people to exist and explore.
